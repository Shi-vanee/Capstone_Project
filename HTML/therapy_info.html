Therapy Approaches

Explore different therapeutic methods to find what might work best for you

Psychotherapy (Talk Therapy)

Overview

Psychotherapy, or talk therapy, is a way to help people with a broad variety of mental illnesses and emotional difficulties. It can help eliminate or control troubling symptoms so a person can function better.

Types

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Focuses on identifying and changing negative thought patterns
  • Psychodynamic Therapy: Focuses on unconscious processes as they are manifested in present behavior
  • Humanistic Therapy: Emphasizes people's capacity to make rational choices
  • Interpersonal Therapy (IPT): Short-term treatment for depression

Process

Typically involves regular sessions (weekly or bi-weekly) where the therapist and client discuss issues and work through problems. The process varies depending on the type of therapy and individual needs.

Conditions Treated

  • Depression
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Eating disorders
  • Addiction
  • Relationship problems
  • Grief or loss

Expectations

Clients can expect a safe, confidential environment to explore feelings, beliefs, or behaviors. The therapist provides guidance without judgment.

Effectiveness

Research shows psychotherapy leads to significant improvement in most people who participate. About 75% of people who enter psychotherapy show some benefit.

Suitability

Psychotherapy can help people of all ages with various mental health conditions. It's particularly effective when combined with medication for certain conditions.

Choosing a Therapist

Consider the therapist's qualifications, experience, approach, and whether you feel comfortable with them. Personal rapport is crucial for success.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Overview

CBT is a structured, time-limited psychotherapy that identifies and changes negative thought patterns and behaviors that contribute to psychological distress.

What It Means

CBT is based on the concept that thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected, and that changing negative thought patterns can lead to changes in feelings and behaviors.

Goal

To teach individuals to become their own therapists by developing coping skills to manage current problems and prevent future relapse.

Uses

  • Depression
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Phobias
  • PTSD
  • Sleep disorders
  • Eating disorders

Techniques

  • Cognitive Restructuring: Identifying and challenging negative thoughts
  • Behavioral Activation: Increasing engagement in positive activities
  • Exposure Therapy: Gradual exposure to feared situations
  • Skills Training: Developing coping strategies

Types

  • Traditional CBT: Standard approach focusing on thoughts and behaviors
  • Mindfulness-Based CBT: Incorporates mindfulness techniques
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): CBT adapted for borderline personality disorder

What to Expect

Typically 5-20 weekly sessions. Homework assignments are common. Focus is on current problems rather than past experiences.

Is It Right for Me?

CBT works well for people who prefer structured, goal-oriented therapy and are willing to actively participate in their treatment.

What to Consider

CBT requires commitment to the process and willingness to examine and challenge your own thoughts. It may be uncomfortable at times as it addresses difficult issues.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

Origins

DBT was developed in the late 1980s by Marsha Linehan to treat borderline personality disorder. It combines CBT principles with mindfulness concepts.

Functionality

DBT helps individuals build a life worth living by balancing acceptance and change strategies. It emphasizes validation while working toward behavioral change.

Skills Taught

  • Mindfulness: Being present in the moment
  • Distress Tolerance: Coping with painful events
  • Emotion Regulation: Managing intense emotions
  • Interpersonal Effectiveness: Maintaining relationships and self-respect

Eligibility

Originally for borderline personality disorder, now used for:

  • Self-harm behaviors
  • Suicidal ideation
  • Substance abuse
  • Eating disorders
  • PTSD

Effectiveness

Research shows DBT reduces self-harm, hospitalizations, and improves social functioning. About 77% of people with BPD no longer meet diagnostic criteria after 1 year of DBT.

DBT vs CBT

While CBT focuses on changing thoughts and behaviors, DBT adds acceptance strategies and places greater emphasis on emotional regulation and the therapeutic relationship.

Finding a Therapist

Look for therapists certified in DBT. Treatment typically includes individual therapy, skills training groups, phone coaching, and therapist consultation teams.
